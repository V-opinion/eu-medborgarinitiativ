Church Elections as a Conflict Resolution Model and the Multi-Religious Election System

Prepared by: De Nya Svenskarna
Target audience: Peace practitioners, policymakers, faith leaders, academics in Sweden and Norway

This brief proposes that the Nordic experience of democratic church elections can inspire a broader Multi-Religious Election System as a concrete reform tool for peacebuilding and conflict prevention, in Europe and globally.

1. Background And Purpose Of The Seminars

Sweden and Norway play a unique role in global peace efforts, including through the Nobel Peace Prize and long-standing engagement in mediation and international dialogue. At the same time, the Nordic region has developed a distinctive practice: democratic elections within Protestant churches.

The purpose of these seminars is to:

  • Present Nordic church elections as a conflict prevention and peace-supporting model.
  • Explore how this model can be translated into a Multi-Religious Election System applicable in diverse contexts.
  • Discuss how such reforms can complement international law and support the separation of state and religion.
  • Engage refugees and international participants as bridges to their countries and communities of origin.

2. The Nordic Model: Positive Aspects Of Church Elections

2.1 Internal Democracy In Religious Communities

In the Nordic countries, Protestant denominations conduct regular elections where members choose their representatives at different levels. This introduces:

  • Democratic governance inside religious communities, not only in state institutions.
  • Accountability of religious leadership to their members.
  • Institutionalized channels for debate and disagreement that reduce pressure for violent confrontation.

2.2 Early Democratic Participation For Young People

In several Nordic contexts, young people from the age of 16 have voting rights in church elections. This:

  • Gives youth practical experience of democracy before general elections.
  • Strengthens democratic culture and responsibility among younger generations.
  • Provides peaceful, legitimate avenues for youth influence within communities of faith.

2.3 Gender Equality In Religious Leadership

Church elections have enabled women to attain the highest leadership positions, including as archbishops. This:

  • Promotes gender equality and challenges exclusionary traditions.
  • Signals that religious institutions can embody human rights and dignity.
  • Broadens ownership of religious institutions among the whole population.
Key insight: Nordic church elections demonstrate that religious communities can be both faithful to their traditions and democratically governed, contributing to social stability and a culture of peaceful conflict resolution.

3. From Church Elections To A Multi-Religious Election System

3.1 Concept

The Multi-Religious Election System (MRES) is a proposed reform where:

  • Different religious communities (Christian, Muslim, Jewish, Buddhist, etc.) develop internal, democratic elections for their governing bodies.
  • These structures are tailored to each tradition but share common principles: transparency, accountability, regular elections, and basic participation rights.
  • The state does not control doctrine but recognizes elected religious bodies as legitimate partners in dialogue and peacebuilding.

3.2 Core Principles Of The MRES

  • Democracy within religion: Elected councils, synods, or boards for major religious communities.
  • Separation of state and religion: The state does not exercise religious authority, and religious institutions do not hold state power.
  • Youth participation: Lower voting ages where possible; education on democracy and non-violence linked to elections.
  • Gender equality and inclusion: Removing barriers for women and marginalized groups to stand as candidates.
  • Inter-religious fairness: Equal opportunity for all major faiths to adopt democratic structures.

3.3 Why This Matters For Conflict Resolution

In many conflicts, religious or identity-based actors claim to speak for entire communities without being elected or accountable. The MRES offers:

  • More legitimate and representative religious leadership in peace processes.
  • Peaceful channels for expressing frustration and negotiating change.
  • A culture where disagreement is managed through procedures, dialogue, and voting instead of violence.

4. Relationship To International Law And Sacred Texts

A common tension in global conflicts is between appeals to sacred texts and commitments under international law. The question “Is it Genesis 12:1 or international law that applies?” symbolizes this dilemma.

The proposed approach recognizes:

  • International law as the binding legal framework for states, protecting people from war, occupation, and state abuse of power.
  • Religious texts and traditions as sources of identity, ethics, and meaning that must be interpreted in ways compatible with peace, human dignity, and coexistence.
  • The MRES as a bridge: democratic religious institutions can interpret sacred texts in dialogue with international norms, reducing the risk that religion is used to justify violence.

5. Proposed Role For Sweden And Norway

5.1 Use Of Nobel And Other Platforms

Sweden and Norway can leverage Nobel-related events, peace research institutes, and universities to:

  • Present Nordic church elections as a case study in peaceful democratization of religion.
  • Host seminars on the Multi-Religious Election System with participants from conflict-affected regions.
  • Encourage joint initiatives between governments, academia, and faith-based organizations.

5.2 Engaging Refugees In The Nordic Countries

Refugees and migrants in the Nordic countries are key actors in this reform idea. Authorities, churches, and civil society can:

  • Inform newly arrived refugees about how church elections work and what advantages and challenges they bring.
  • Support refugees in discussing whether similar democratic mechanisms could be adapted to their own religious traditions.
  • Encourage them to act as messengers to compatriots and communities, both in the diaspora and in their countries of origin.

5.3 Stepwise European Expansion

After consolidating and documenting experiences in Denmark, Sweden, and Finland, the next step is to:

  • Organize EU-level discussions on religious democracy and conflict prevention.
  • Explain why only some EU countries currently practice church elections and explore interest in broader adoption.
  • Develop guidelines for how democratic religious structures could be recognized and supported across the EU.

6. Relevance For Current Conflicts

While no single reform can end complex conflicts, the MRES can complement diplomatic and legal efforts in, for example:

  • Israel–Palestine and surrounding countries.
  • Sudan, Somalia, Yemen, Libya, Nigeria.
  • Myanmar, the Korean peninsula, and others.
  • Conflicts involving Russia and Ukraine.

In such contexts, elected religious bodies could:

  • Provide representative interlocutors for peace negotiations.
  • Help ensure that long-standing conflicts are not transmitted unchanged to the next generation.
  • Promote narratives of reconciliation grounded in both faith and democratic legitimacy.

7. Suggested Discussion Questions For The Seminars

  • Which elements of Nordic church elections are universal, and which are specific to the Nordic Protestant context?
  • How can democratic elections be designed in different religious traditions without violating their core beliefs?
  • What safeguards are needed to prevent state control over religion while still supporting democratic reforms?
  • How can refugees and diasporas be supported as agents of peaceful religious reform in their home regions?
  • What are concrete first pilot projects for a Multi-Religious Election System in or around current conflict zones?

8. Conclusion

The Nordic experience with church elections shows that democracy, youth participation, and gender equality can be successfully integrated into religious institutions. A carefully designed Multi-Religious Election System could extend these benefits beyond the Nordic region, strengthening the separation of state and religion while making religious leadership more accountable, inclusive, and peace-oriented.

Sweden and Norway are well placed to initiate this conversation and to host comparative discussions that bring together international law experts, religious leaders, refugees, and peace practitioners to develop this model further and test it in practice.
