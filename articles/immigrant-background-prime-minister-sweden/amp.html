Making It Possible for a Politician With an Immigrant Background to Become Prime Minister

Why is it still so difficult for politicians with an immigrant background to claim fundamental rights for themselves and others, and to reach the highest political office in Sweden?

The Question of Who May Become Prime Minister

In a democracy, the office of prime minister should, in principle, be open to every citizen who meets the formal legal requirements and gains the trust of the electorate. Yet in practice, the post often appears reserved for a very narrow group: ethnically Swedish, Protestant politicians who follow the traditional party line in the church election and embody the majority culture.

For politicians with an immigrant background, the message can seem implicit but clear: to be fully accepted, you must resemble the traditional Swedish Protestant norm as closely as possible. In the most extreme interpretation, it is almost as if conversion to Protestant Christianity is perceived as the safest path to full political legitimacy and eligibility for the role of prime minister.

Why Are Politicians With Immigrant Backgrounds Weak in Defending Fundamental Rights?

Many politicians with immigrant backgrounds enter politics with strong personal experiences of discrimination, exclusion, or authoritarian societies. One might therefore expect them to be especially strong defenders of fundamental rights. Instead, they are often perceived as cautious, quiet, or unwilling to challenge deep structural injustices, including those linked to religion and identity.

There are several possible reasons:

  • Fear of being seen as ungrateful or "difficult": Criticising the majority culture or its institutions can easily be framed as attacking Sweden itself. Many prefer to avoid that risk.
  • Dependence on party leadership: To advance in party structures, one is expected to follow the line, not to push controversial questions about religion, minority rights or church elections.
  • Lack of knowledge about rights and church structures: The connection between the Church of Sweden, party politics and how it shapes legitimacy is complex. Ignorance exists not only among voters but also among politicians, including those with immigrant backgrounds.
  • Internalised norms: After many years in the system, even critical voices can internalise the idea that challenging the religious-cultural norm is "too radical" or "not strategic".

The result is paradoxical: those who could be the strongest advocates for equal rights and genuine pluralism often become the most careful in exactly those areas where change is most needed.

Conversion Cannot Be the Ticket to Power

When it is subtly communicated that party leaders or high-profile politicians with immigrant backgrounds would be more "acceptable" if they converted to Protestant Christianity, something is deeply wrong in the democratic culture. The idea that, for example, a leader of V or L with a Muslim, secular, or other religious background must convert in order to be truly eligible as Swedish prime minister is completely incompatible with modern human rights and equality.

In a secular democracy, the following should apply:

  • The state must be neutral in religious matters.
  • Parties must not act as gatekeepers for a specific confession.
  • Religious affiliation, or lack thereof, must not be an informal requirement for the highest political offices.

As long as it is perceived that the prime minister must be a Swedish Protestant who fits into an unspoken cultural-religious template, the system is not truly inclusive. It becomes almost impossible for many citizens with immigrant backgrounds to imagine themselves in that position, regardless of competence or support.

Lessons From Women's Long Road to the Prime Ministership

Swedish women gained the right to vote in 1921. Yet it took more than a hundred years before a woman finally became prime minister: Magdalena Andersson (S). Her time in office was short, around seven months, before power shifted and the Sweden Democrats gained a central role in Swedish politics. For many, this felt like a double setback:

  • A symbolic breakthrough for women at the very top of politics was immediately followed by a political shift strengthening a party with a restrictive approach to immigration and diversity.
  • The brief tenure risked being interpreted as proof that women's leadership at the highest level remains fragile and conditional.

For women with immigrant backgrounds, the signal is even more discouraging. If it took over a century for a majority-ethnic Swedish woman to reach the prime ministership, how long will it take for a woman with an immigrant background – or a man with an immigrant background – to achieve the same?

History shows that formal rights are not enough. Women had the vote for decades before equal representation at the top became even remotely realistic. The same pattern may repeat for citizens with immigrant backgrounds: having the right to vote and to stand for office is one thing; being seen as a fully legitimate prime ministerial candidate is something else.

The Role of the Church Election and Cultural Christianity

In Sweden, the church election continues to link party politics to the Church of Sweden. Participation, allegiance to the party line, and a culturally Protestant identity still play an invisible role in who is considered "one of us" in the deepest sense. This has consequences:

  • Politicians who are openly non-Christian, Muslim, secular or belong to another faith tradition are easily perceived as more "foreign".
  • Even when citizenship and competence are not in question, cultural Christianity becomes a kind of informal loyalty test.
  • Many politicians with immigrant backgrounds underestimate how much the church election and its norms shape perceptions of who can be trusted with ultimate political power.
Ignorance about the church election and its political implications affects not only the general public but also elected representatives. Without knowledge, they cannot demand reforms that separate political legitimacy from a specific religious tradition.

What Must Change?

To make it genuinely possible for a politician with an immigrant background to become prime minister, several parallel changes are needed – in institutions, parties, and public consciousness.

1. Clarify and Defend Secular Principles

The idea that the prime minister "should" be Protestant must be challenged openly. Parties, media and civil society must defend the principle that religious affiliation is irrelevant to eligibility for the highest office, as long as the candidate respects the constitution and democratic values.

2. Reform the Relationship Between Party Politics and the Church

As long as the church election is strongly party-politicised, informal religious-cultural loyalty tests will remain in the background. A clearer separation between church and political parties would make it harder to link political trust to a specific confession.

3. Strengthen Rights Awareness Among Politicians With Immigrant Backgrounds

Politicians with immigrant backgrounds need knowledge and tools to confidently invoke fundamental rights – for themselves and for others. This includes understanding:

  • anti-discrimination laws,
  • freedom of religion and belief,
  • international human rights conventions,
  • how religious norms can influence political structures.

4. Encourage Open Debate Inside the Parties

Parties must create space for internal critique of informal norms. It must be acceptable to say: "Why is it taken for granted that the prime minister should be a certain type of Swede, with a certain religious background?" Without such debate, hidden barriers are never removed – they are only denied.

5. Change Narratives in Media and Public Discourse

Journalists and opinion leaders play a key role. If media constantly presents only one kind of person as a natural prime minister, other groups will be seen as exceptions or "risky experiments". We need new narratives and examples that normalise the idea of a prime minister with an immigrant background.

From Token Inclusion to Real Possibility

Today, Sweden can point to many politicians with immigrant backgrounds in parliament and government. Yet representation at the very top remains absent. As long as no one with an immigrant background has become prime minister, the system signals a limit – an invisible ceiling of trust and legitimacy.

The goal must be to move from token inclusion – where people with immigrant backgrounds are visible but rarely in decisive positions – to real possibility, where it is completely normal to imagine such a person as head of government.

Just as women's long struggle for the prime ministership shows that formal rights are not enough, the struggle for equal opportunities for citizens with immigrant backgrounds will require time, pressure, and courage. But the alternative – a democracy where only one cultural-religious group is truly eligible for the highest office – is not worthy of a modern Sweden.

Making it possible for a politician with an immigrant background to be elected prime minister is therefore not a special interest issue. It is a test of how seriously Sweden takes its own democratic promises of equality, freedom of religion, and the equal dignity of all citizens.
